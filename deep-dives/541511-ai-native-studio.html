AI-Native Services Studio

From 4-digit aggregate (5415) into 6-digit specialization (541511) · Cross-vertical roll-up sub-thesis · Islands operator case (Ali El-Shayeb)
4-digit (5415): Hell-Yes → 6-digit (541511): Hell-Yes

Both the 4-digit aggregate (5415 Computer Systems Design and Related Services) and the 6-digit specialization (541511 Custom Computer Programming Services) score Hell-Yes on the main map. 5415 is the second-highest-scoring sector on the entire map (ΣS=193,200, 8 Hell-Yes cells — even denser than 5412 accounting), driven by direct AI-on-AI productivity gains: software developers building software with AI tools is the canonical use case. Within 541511, the investable opportunity is the AI-native services studio sub-thesis — operator-led firms that run a high-margin AI engineering services book, then use that infrastructure to acquire and re-platform adjacent verticals (marketing agencies, content production, fractional CTO services) where AI compresses costs even more dramatically. Islands (Ali El-Shayeb, Toronto; 32 people across Canada / Brazil / Moldova / Netherlands / Philippines / Egypt; $5.5-6M revenue at 40% EBITDA / $80-120K monthly cashflow, bootstrapped from $20K) is the operator demonstrating the model — with the additional structural moat of Canadian SR&ED tax credits stacking to 60%+ of tech salaries (verified externally), a cross-border arbitrage that no US-based competitor can replicate.

NAICS structure

Per US Census 2022 NAICS, the 4-digit industry group 5415 Computer Systems Design and Related Services contains four 6-digit codes:

  • 541511 Custom Computer Programming Services — software development on a contract or project basis (Islands' core revenue)
  • 541512 Computer Systems Design Services — architecture, integration, systems planning
  • 541513 Computer Facilities Management Services — outsourced IT operations / MSP-adjacent
  • 541519 Other Computer Related Services — data recovery, disaster recovery, niche IT services

All four share the Hell-Yes drivers — recurring project + retainer revenue, real AI productivity lever (LLMs writing code, generating tests, refactoring, drafting docs), and massive fragmentation. 5415 has the highest sum-S (193,200) we score and 8 cells above the Hell-Yes threshold of 12,000 — more than any other sector. 541511 specifically is where AI-native services studios operate: fractional CTO work, AI engineering pods, custom AI agent development, n8n / LangChain workflow builds. The unit economics are unique because the operator both uses AI internally (cost compression) and sells AI deployment to clients (revenue expansion).

The operator-led AI services pattern. This is the same Adam Zoia / Glocap / Sam Young / Level CFO framing applied to programming-as-a-service: software at $99-$300/mo cannot replace humans for SMB execution; clients want answers and deliverables, not dashboards. AI-native services studios sell senior engineers with AI internally instead of selling AI tools to senior engineers. The pricing wedge is dramatic — Islands delivers AI content agency service at $1.5-2.5K/mo where traditional marketing agencies charge ~$10K/mo. The same delivery is achieved with 8 AI agents on n8n workflows handling research → strategy → execution.

Why both bands are Hell-Yes

1. Direct AI-on-AI productivity gains

5415 has the highest aggregate AI productivity score on the entire map (ΣS=193,200, 8 Hell-Yes cells). The workflow content — write code, generate tests, refactor, draft documentation, build integrations — is the canonical use case for LLMs. McKinsey developer productivity 2024: 35-55% reduction in time-on-task for routine engineering work; GitHub Copilot internal studies: 30-40% productivity gain across the developer cohort.

2. Recurring services revenue priced on talent, not tools

Fractional CTO engagements: $5-15K/month per client. AI engineering pods: $20-50K/month. Project work: $50-500K per project. Revenue compounds with the talent supply, not with software subscriptions. Operator captures the AI productivity gain as margin rather than passing it through as a cheaper SaaS price.

3. Massive fragmentation — 125,645 US firms in 5415

Per US Census CBP 2023: 125,645 firms in NAICS 5415 with $4.78M average revenue. Boutique AI agencies, dev shops, and consulting firms number in the tens of thousands. Top 10 IT services firms (Accenture, Infosys, TCS, etc.) hold meaningful share at enterprise top end but the SMB and mid-market boutique tier is structurally fragmented.

4. Verified +8.5 pp AI Δ on the main map — second-highest in sector 54

The main rollup-economy map computes 5415's AI margin uplift at +8.5 percentage points (only 5412 accounting at +7.8 is comparable; everything else in sector 54 is below). This is a function of the work content — code generation is what LLMs were trained to do best.

What Islands actually does — three layers

Fractional CTO work for non-technical founders. AI Squad engineering pods that build software. AI content agency (research → strategy → execution via 8 AI agents on n8n workflows). Pricing wedge: $1.5-2.5K/mo for AI content vs $10K traditional marketing agency rates. ~25 invoices per month to active service clients.

Layer 2 — Venture studio (30% of revenue)

~4 portfolio ventures, all profitable. Each spawned from solving a real client problem and then commercialized. Sub-venture Shoreline: Canadian tech-talent placement, monetizes the SR&ED arbitrage for clients via outsourced hiring (engineer paid CAD, billed USD, government refund stacked).

Target acquisitions: marketing agencies with $5M revenue / $1M EBITDA / 50-100 active clients each. 3 acquisitions in 2026 = 100-200 served clients. The Canadian SR&ED tax arbitrage — verified moat

Canada's Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED) program refunds a significant fraction of qualified R&D salaries. Verified externally:

Federal SR&ED (CCPC enhanced)35%100% refundable on first $3M qualified expenditureCanada Revenue Agency

Combined federal + provincial stacking can return 60%+ of eligible technical salaries in Quebec (most aggressive), 43-45% in Ontario / BC. Ali's worked example matches the math:

  • Pay Canadian engineer $100K salary
  • Bill US client $220K (standard agency markup ~2.2×)
  • End of fiscal year: government refunds ~$60K via SR&ED
  • Net effective margin per engineer: ~$180K (revenue $220K less salary $100K + $60K refund)
  • Plus standard agency overhead, this still produces 40%+ EBITDA

CCPC eligibility constraint — the structural moat. Only Canadian-Controlled Private Corporations (CCPCs) get the 35% enhanced rate AND the 100% refundability. Requirement: ≥50% Canadian-resident ownership of voting shares and ≥50% Canadian-owned IP. Islands is structured as a Canadian holding company with a Delaware subsidiary for US client servicing — preserves CCPC eligibility while accessing US deal flow (90% of customers US-based). A US-based AI services studio cannot replicate this without restructuring under Canadian ownership. Ali is also an advisor to the Canadian government on AI grants for small companies, which adds additional credit-stacking opportunities on top of base SR&ED.

The cross-vertical roll-up thesis — Hell-Yes operator into Conditional target

Islands operates in Hell-Yes 5415 (Computer Systems Design). The acquisition target is 5418 Advertising, Public Relations, and Related Services — which scores Conditional on the main map (ΣS=20,916, maxS=3,780). This is a deliberate cross-vertical play: take a Hell-Yes operator's AI infrastructure and apply it to a Conditional vertical that is in active disruption.

🟡 Why 5418 (marketing agencies) is Conditional

Aggregate 5418 EBITDA 28.3%, AI Δ +3.4 pp. Healthy on paper but in active AI disruption: traditional content production, copywriting, and SEO can be produced at 10-20× lower cost with generative AI. Established agencies are losing accounts; revenue trajectory is declining for many. The verdict is held in Conditional band by relationship stickiness (close coupling to client sales orgs) and brand premium (top-tier agencies still command Fortune-500 retainers), but the long tail is structurally pressured.

🟢 Why this makes them ideal acquisition targets

Declining revenue trajectory = compressed entry multiples (3-5× EBITDA for boutique agencies in 2025-2026, down from 6-9× pre-AI). Sticky client relationships = retained book at acquisition. AI replacement of production work = immediate margin expansion. Islands' n8n + 8-AI-agent stack can deliver agency output at $1.5-2.5K/mo where the acquired agency was charging $10K/mo — the operator either captures the price spread or compresses cost to preserve margin while keeping price.

Top risks

R1 — First-time consolidator execution risk

Ali's explicit admission: never done a roll-up. The operational, legal, and financial mechanics of multi-firm acquisition + integration are non-trivial. Without an experienced M&A partner (S16VC's value-add) or hired Chief Integration Officer, the first 2 acquisitions are at execution risk. Diligence: rate of integration playbook development; key hires before second acquisition closes.

R2 — SR&ED program policy risk

SR&ED rates and refundability have changed historically; 2024 budget reviews considered narrowing CCPC enhanced refundability. If federal program were reduced from 35% to 25% (or refundability removed), the arbitrage compresses by ~30%. The structural moat depends on a single legislative program. Diligence: review pending federal budget proposals; stress-test base economics without SR&ED uplift.

R3 — Client retention through ownership change

Marketing agency client relationships are typically built on individual account-manager trust, not institutional contracts. Acquisition can trigger client departures (industry norm: 15-30% within Y1). Re-platforming to AI delivery — while internally cost-saving — can be perceived by clients as "you're replacing my account team with bots." Mitigation requires careful retention of senior account-manager talent + transparent communication of AI-augmented (not AI-replaced) delivery.

R4 — Operator-led thesis crowding

The "AI-native services studio rolling up adjacent verticals" thesis is exactly what Pioneers (Leo Shangin), Fonzi AI (Drew Moffitt), Chassi (Shane Penner) are pursuing across services categories. Islands has the SR&ED structural moat but otherwise competes for the same boutique acquisition targets in marketing. First-mover advantage on PE capital and proprietary deal flow matters in the 18-24 month window.
