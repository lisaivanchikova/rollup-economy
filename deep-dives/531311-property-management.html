Residential Property Management

From 4-digit aggregate (5313) into 6-digit specialization (531311) · Middle-market PM consolidation · Outpost Group / Polenko Holding operator case
4-digit (5313): Hell-Yes → 6-digit (531311): Hell-Yes

Both the 4-digit aggregate (5313 Activities Related to Real Estate) and the 6-digit specialization (531311 Residential Property Managers) score Hell-Yes on the main map. They share the same structural drivers — fee-based recurring revenue, asset-light operating model, real AI productivity lever on back-office work, and a highly fragmented operator base (~326,000 US PM companies, top 50 control only 16% of units) with baby-boomer succession pressure forcing supply onto the market. The middle-market tier (500–10,000 units per operator) is the consolidation window: too large for the SMB long tail's economics, too small for the national giants (Greystar, FirstService, Cushman & Wakefield) to bother with. Outpost Group / Polenko Holding (CEO Sergii Starostin) is a textbook execution of this thesis — built through four sequential acquisitions of failed VC-backed PropTech (Distress 2019, Quarters 2021, Common Living 2024, John Holmes 2025), now combining ~4,000 units across NYC/Boston/DC/Texas with an AI-native PM platform and a forever-free software strategy that turns the operating business into a customer-acquisition channel for adjacent transaction monetization.

NAICS structure

Per US Census 2022 NAICS, the 4-digit industry group 5313 Activities Related to Real Estate contains the following 6-digit codes:

  • 531311 Residential Property Managers — managing residential properties for third-party owners on a fee basis
  • 531312 Nonresidential Property Managers — same model but for office/retail/industrial property
  • 531320 Offices of Real Estate Appraisers
  • 531390 Other Activities Related to Real Estate — escrow, fiduciary, listing services, advisory

The 4-digit aggregate scores Hell-Yes because 531311 + 531312 (the two PM legs) carry most of the firm count and revenue, both share strong fee-economics structure, and the formula multiplier M is high (4) — PM is a service business priced on EBITDA multiples, not an asset business priced on cap rate. The colors match Hell-Yes → Hell-Yes.

Important distinction. 531311 (Property Managers) is a fee-for-service business — operator collects rent on behalf of landlord, keeps a management fee (typically 5-10% of collections + leasing fee + ancillary), passes through the rest. The operator never owns the asset and never holds the lease liability. This is structurally different from 531110 (Lessors of Residential Buildings) where the operator owns or master-leases the unit and carries occupancy risk. Master-lease co-living models (Common, Sonder, Selina, Quarters, etc.) live in 531110; legitimate PM consolidators like Outpost live in 531311. The same operator can shift between codes over its lifecycle — Outpost itself began as a master-lease arbitrage business in 2019 and pivoted to property management during COVID after 90% of its landlords agreed to convert from master-lease to fee-based PM contracts.

Why both bands are Hell-Yes

1. Fee-based recurring revenue, asset-light

Standard PM fee: 5-10% of monthly rent collected + tenant placement (typically one month's rent) + lease renewal + ancillary (maintenance markup, late fees). Recurring, automatic, low capex. No master-lease obligation = no occupancy risk transferred to operator.

2. Massive fragmentation + succession supply

~326,000 US PM companies (B2B Reviews / DoorLoop 2024 data) serving 19.34M rental properties. Top 50 firms control only ~3.1M units (16% of market). 220,000+ companies are sub-500 unit operators, most owner-operated by baby boomers heading into retirement. Acquisition supply is structurally large and growing.

3. Real AI productivity lever on back-office work

PM workflow is mostly information handling — leasing, scheduling, invoicing, maintenance dispatch, tenant communication, owner reporting. Per Sergii Starostin's operating data: one leasing agent handles ~7 leads/day pre-AI; target is 50 leads/day post-AI (5-7× productivity). Outpost target by end-2026: 80% AI-handled lease/sales tickets, 60% AI-handled customer service tickets (currently ~20% of 10-12K monthly tickets).

4. Service business priced on EBITDA — wide multiple arbitrage

Unlike asset-owning lessors (531110) who trade on cap rates, PM operators trade on EBITDA multiples. Small PM (1-500 units): 3-5× EBITDA. Mid-market PM ($1M+ EBITDA): 6-9×. Strategic platform with technology + national footprint: 10-12×+. The arbitrage between tuck-in entry and platform exit is exactly what the roll-up math needs.

The middle-market consolidation window

US PM companies stratify into three operator tiers by units under management:

🟡 SMB tier (1-500 units)

~220,000 companies, most owner-operated. Owner makes $50-200K annual income from PM fees. Below the unit-economics floor for paid marketing channels. Not viable as standalone targets for institutional roll-up; viable as bolt-ons within a city where the platform already has anchor presence.

🟢 Middle market (500-10,000 units)

The consolidation target. Above the marketing-channel break-even (Sergii Starostin: ≥800 units in a single city to recoup Costar/Apartments.com/Zillow fixed monthly spend ~$10K). Below the scale where national giants compete. Owner usually founder-operated, often with succession question. AI productivity lever applies most clearly to this tier (largest operational waste, weakest existing tech stack).

🟡 Giant tier (10K+ units)

~50 companies: Greystar (~840K units globally), FirstService Residential, Cushman & Wakefield, RealPage, Asset Living. Public or PE-backed at scale. Trade at premium multiples (10-12× EBITDA for residential-focused). Not acquisition targets for the consolidator; potentially strategic exit buyers.

Sergii's macro thesis on PropTech failures. "VC-backed PropTech companies systematically break because the VC goal — grow at all costs — is incompatible with real estate economics, where the cost of a mistake sits 20 years. In real estate you grow when there's a good deal, not when you need to push." Outpost's strategy is to be the patient, unit-economics-disciplined consolidator that picks up the distressed assets at near-zero acquisition cost (failed competitors absorbed via direct landlord re-contracting rather than asset purchase). The four-acquisition track record validates the mechanism — and the underlying PM business model is structurally different from the master-lease businesses that collapsed.

The differentiating bet — AI-native PM software + forever-free strategy

The market is dominated by four legacy PM platforms — AppFolio, Buildium, Yardi, Entrata — covering ~90% of US PM companies. None are AI-native; all sit on top of pre-LLM data structures and workflows. Outpost is building a parallel AI-native stack with the explicit intent to give it away.

The strategy

Build integrations with AppFolio, Buildium, Yardi, Entrata. Migrate their customers to Outpost's software at zero cost. Monetize via (a) transaction fees on rent collection, leasing, insurance, maintenance, and 3rd-party services; (b) financial-data visibility into thousands of PM businesses → proprietary signal for M&A acquisition targeting.

Why it works

PM operators hate paying SaaS fees ($150-2,000+/month). Free software at parity-or-better functionality dislodges the legacy installed base. Once on Outpost's platform, the operator's customer base, financials, and unit economics are visible — feeding both the transaction monetization layer and the M&A pipeline (Outpost knows which operators are profitable, distressed, or succession-ready before they go to market).

Open question. Whether the free-software-as-acquisition-funnel becomes a stand-alone integration business. Sergii's view: at 5-10K units of external visibility it's not worth spinning off; at 100K+ external units the data + transaction layer becomes its own business. Decision deferred until scale supports it.

Top risks

R1 — Forward EBITDA execution risk

Q-of-E audit confirms forward $4.5M but Q1 2026 actuals are still loss-making. If the John Holmes integration takes longer than projected (cost cuts back-loaded, contract terminations slower than expected, severance higher than budgeted), the forward 12-mo run rate compresses materially. Diligence: month-by-month bridge from current ($454K) Q1 to projected $1.1M+ Q4 must be specific and dated.

R2 — Master-lease tail exposure

The "Net Rental Revenue" line at $5.4M T12 (42% of revenue) suggests legacy master-lease portfolio still exists alongside the PM book. Master-lease economics carry occupancy risk and lease obligations regardless of tenant fill (the structural problem that killed Common, Sonder, Selina). Diligence: what % of OJH's revenue is true fee-based PM vs residual master-lease pass-through? What is the remaining lease tail in months and dollars?

R3 — AI-native software execution

The forever-free PM software thesis depends on Outpost actually building functional AI-native replacement for AppFolio/Buildium/Yardi/Entrata — a 20-year-old product category with deep moats. First eval system stats due end-May 2026 (per CTO Zhenya). If the AI agents miss the 5× productivity target, the strategy collapses to "another regional PM consolidator" rather than "Hell-Yes AI-native platform."

R4 — Strategic exit channel

Strategic acquirers at the giant tier (Greystar, FirstService, Cushman & Wakefield, RealPage) buy by units, not by tech narratives — they want operating book + people, not software stacks they will replace. PE secondary exit at 10-12× EBITDA requires demonstrated scale (>$5M EBITDA platform) and tech that PE recognizes as defensible. Outpost can probably reach scale; the tech-defensibility narrative is harder.

Final thesis

4-digit and 6-digit verdicts match: both 5313 and 531311 are Hell-Yes. The aggregate is carried by 531311 (Residential PM) + 531312 (Nonresidential PM), both sharing fee-based recurring revenue, asset-light economics, real AI productivity lever, and massive fragmentation (~326K US PM companies, top 50 control only 16% of units). Middle-market PM (500-10K units per operator) is the consolidation window — above the SMB marketing break-even, below the giant-tier competition. Outpost Group / Polenko Holding (CEO Sergii Starostin) is a textbook execution of the Hell-Yes PM thesis: four successive M&A integrations of failed VC-backed PropTech, pivot from master-lease to fee-based PM in 2020, ~4,000 units under management, 160-person multi-country team, AI-native software stack in build, forever-free distribution strategy. Financials are mid-turnaround: T12 actuals still EBITDA-negative ($925K), but Q1-to-Q4 trajectory clearly positive and independent Q-of-E confirms +$4.5M forward 12-month EBITDA. Series A at $50-60M valuation (~11-13× forward EBITDA) closes by end-summer 2026. The thesis works if the forward EBITDA bridge executes as audited and the master-lease residual is small or shrinking — both diligenceable. This is the right vertical, executed by an operator who has lived its full cycle (master-lease arbitrage → PM pivot → consolidator) and learned the unit economics the hard way.
