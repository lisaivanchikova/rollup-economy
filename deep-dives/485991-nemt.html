NEMT — Non-Emergency Medical Transportation

From 4-digit aggregate (4859) into 6-digit specialization (485991) · Hybrid Ride, Inc. operator case
4-digit (4859): Pass → 6-digit (485991): Conditional · selective thesis

The 4-digit aggregate (4859 Other Transit and Ground Passenger Transportation) scores Pass — distribution and moat are weak across the broader sub-sector, with 485999 (employee shuttles, airport shuttles, miscellaneous local transit) dragging the aggregate into the red band. The 6-digit specialization (485991 Special Needs Transportation) is structurally better than its aggregate: federal Medicaid mandate, recurring revenue, real operator-level fragmentation. But it does not reach Strong — the 2025 bankruptcy of the largest public NEMT operator (Modivcare), Medicare Advantage transportation benefit cuts for 2026, intensifying federal fraud enforcement, and a single-strategic-acquirer exit market hold the sub-segment in the Conditional band, investable only under a tightly defined selective thesis.

NAICS structure

Per US Census 2022 NAICS, the 4-digit industry group 4859 Other Transit and Ground Passenger Transportation contains exactly two 6-digit codes:

  • 485991 Special Needs Transportation (Medicaid NEMT, dialysis transport, school-age special-needs, paratransit subcontracted to ADA programs)
  • 485999 All Other Transit and Ground Passenger Transportation (airport shuttle, employee shuttle, miscellaneous local transit, gondolas, aerial trams)

School bus is in 4854 (separate industry group), charter bus is in 4855 (separate industry group). They are not included in 4859. The drag on the 4859 aggregate comes from 485999, which mixes low-margin commodity shuttles with miscellaneous transit and has weaker recurring-revenue characteristics than NEMT.

Note on Hybrid Ride's own projection (26.3% EBITDA). Their Excel models a post-AI state at 33 vehicles (a fleet expansion of +3 cars), with G&A cut as if already operating inside a multi-operator platform (Ops Manager $7K → $2K, Local Dispatchers $16K → $4K). So their 26.3% is a hybrid of (a) standalone AI gains + (b) scale-up to 33 cars + (c) shared-services assumption that depends on being part of a roll-up. 4-digit (4859) vs 6-digit (485991) — verdict comparison

🔴 4-digit (4859) — Pass

The 4859 aggregate scores Pass on the main map. Distribution and moat collapse across the broader sub-sector because 485999 dominates the firm count and revenue mix:

  • Discretionary corporate spend (not federally mandated)
  • Low recurring-revenue characteristics
  • Commodity pricing in shuttle markets
  • Limited tech-leverage on small fleets
  • No structural pricing power vs. corporate buyers

The aggregate-level S score lands in the Pass band (33-170) because the multiplicative formula penalizes the weak C (distribution + moat) of the dominant 485999 sub-segment.

🟡 6-digit (485991) — Conditional, selective thesis

485991 has structurally better characteristics than the 4859 aggregate, breaking up one verdict band — from Pass into Conditional — but does not reach Strong:

  • + Federal Medicaid mandate (42 CFR 431.53, SSA §1902(a)(87))
  • + Operator-level fragmentation (thousands of independents)
  • + Recurring Medicaid-anchored revenue
  • − Modivcare (largest public NEMT operator) entered Chapter 11 in 2025
  • − Medicare Advantage transportation benefits cut for 2026
  • − Broker market consolidated into MTM + Modivcare-post-Ch11
  • − Single credible strategic acquirer (MTM); no MA payer NEMT M&A on record

The positives lift the verdict one band above the aggregate; the structural headwinds prevent it climbing further into Strong.

Conclusion of the 4-to-6 drill-down: the colors differ by one band. The red 4-digit aggregate hides an amber 6-digit sub-segment. This is the case the deep-dive layer exists to surface — an industry that screens out at the aggregate level but contains a structurally better specialization that is worth selective consideration.

Market environment 2025-2026

⚠️ Structural headwinds (primary-source verified)

  • Modivcare Chapter 11 — filed Aug 20, 2025 (S.D. Texas, Case 25-90309); emerged Dec 29, 2025; common stock canceled, ~$1.1B debt eliminated, secured lenders received equity. Q3 2025 NEMT segment operating margin: 3.8%. (Source: SEC 10-Q Q3 2025; Ch11 emergence 8-K Dec 26, 2025)
  • Medicare Advantage transportation benefit cuts — KFF data via HMA April 2025 report: MA plans offering transportation benefits dropped from 36% in 2024 to 24% in 2026. UnitedHealthcare, Humana, and Aetna all scaled back 2026 plans. (Source: KFF, Healthcare Dive on 2026 MA plans)
  • CMS NEMT provider enrollment moratorium — In effect since Oct 2023; Colorado extended through Sept 30, 2026. Limits organic network growth in affected states. (Source: CMS provider enrollment guidance)
  • Federal fraud enforcement intensifying — June 30, 2025: NY AG charged/settled with 25 NEMT providers; $13M+ paid back to NY Medicaid. June 6, 2025 Presidential Memo "Eliminating Waste, Fraud, and Abuse in Medicaid." (Source: NY AG press release)
  • State carve-in trend — Michigan moved NEMT into MCO model effective Oct 1, 2024 (eliminated independent broker tier); Colorado consolidating to single statewide broker. (Source: HMA April 2025 NEMT report)
  • Broker market consolidated — MTM acquired Veyo (Aug 2022) and Access2Care from Global Medical Response (Oct 2024). MTM + Modivcare-post-Ch11 coordinate the majority of brokered Medicaid NEMT. Broker-level roll-up is closed; only the operator tier remains fragmented. (Source: MTM press releases)
  • AI dispatch ROI is thinly documented — Vendor case studies are mostly anonymized, self-attested, qualitative. Modivcare's WellRyde acquisition (May 2021, $30M) has not disclosed quantified ROI in 4 years of subsequent SEC filings. (Source: Routific blog, RouteGenie case studies, Modivcare 10-K series)

Five-year platform math

If 10 operators of Hybrid Ride's size are acquired at sub-$1M EBITDA tuck-in multiples (3-4×) and combined into a platform using Scenario B per-operator EBITDA of ~$869K:

  • Aggregate platform EBITDA: ~$8.7M (10 × $869K post-integration)
  • Entry capital deployed: ~$25-32M (10 × pre-AI EBITDA ~$470K × 3.5-4×)
  • Exit multiple at platform scale: 6-8× (Modivcare's bankruptcy compressed the comparable; surviving NEMT operator multiples clear lower than healthy-era benchmarks)
  • Exit enterprise value: $52-70M
  • Estimated MoIC: 1.6-2.2× over a 4-5 year hold

This MoIC range assumes a healthy exit market. If MA transportation benefit erosion continues through 2027-2028, NEMT operator multiples compress further; downside MoIC could be 1.2-1.6×. For a $50M fund, NEMT should be sized as a small position with tight covenants, not a flagship roll-up.

What would have to be true for this to work

✅ Conditions that make the selective thesis investable

  • Focus on specialty sub-segments — wheelchair, stretcher, dialysis routes — where Uber/Lyft cannot compete and per-trip pricing is structurally higher
  • Direct MCO contracts that bypass broker pricing — operators capture 100% of trip economics rather than broker-dictated subcontract rates
  • Regional density in 1-2 states with stable Medicaid programs (avoid MI carve-in jurisdictions; avoid states transitioning to single-broker models)
  • Conservative entry multiples (3-3.5×) — no premium even for desirable assets
  • <5-year exit window — exit before MA transportation cuts compound
  • Platform back-office that genuinely realizes Scenario B G&A economics (not just claimed)

❌ Conditions that would invalidate the thesis

  • Premium entry multiples (>4×) — no margin for execution risk
  • Ambulatory-only fleet competing against Uber Health / Lyft Healthcare
  • Single-state concentration in states with carve-in or single-broker transitions
  • Leverage above 3-3.5× EBITDA — Modivcare's Ch11 lesson
  • Dependence on MA supplemental benefit growth as tailwind (it is reversing)
  • Exit dependent on Modivcare or UnitedHealth/Optum as buyer (the first is post-Ch11 capital-constrained; the second has no NEMT M&A history)

Adjacency to HomeCare (6216)

NEMT shares the Medicaid payer ecosystem and the elderly/disabled customer base with HomeCare (6216). The strategic logic for combining the two on one platform — bidding for combined MCO contracts — is real, and UnitedHealth's 2023 acquisition of LHC Group ($5.4B) shows payer interest in HomeCare specifically. However: HomeCare also scores Conditional; UnitedHealth's NEMT integration with Modivcare is a partnership, not acquisition; and there is no record of an MA payer acquiring an NEMT operator. A combined HomeCare + NEMT platform thesis depends on the HomeCare leg, not on NEMT being acquired by a payer.

Final thesis

4-digit and 6-digit colors differ by one band: 4859 is Pass, 485991 is Conditional. The 6-digit specialization (485991) is structurally better than its 485999 sibling — federal mandate, recurring Medicaid revenue, real operator-level fragmentation — which lifts the verdict one band above the aggregate, but not better enough to climb into Strong. Hybrid Ride's current 13% EBITDA is consistent with historical operator economics. Independent post-AI estimates: ~16% standalone (Scenario A, +3 pp · triangulated), ~25% with 10-operator roll-up (Scenario B, +11 pp). Platform math: 3-4× entry → 6-8× exit yields ~3-4× spread, generating 1.6-2.2× MoIC over a 4-5 year hold under healthy exit-market assumptions. The Modivcare 2025 Chapter 11, Medicare Advantage transportation benefit cuts for 2026, intensifying federal fraud enforcement, and the consolidation of the broker market into a single dominant strategic acquirer (MTM) all support a cautious sizing. Worth pursuing only as a specialty/regional operator roll-up with direct MCO contracts and conservative leverage — not as a flagship platform position.
